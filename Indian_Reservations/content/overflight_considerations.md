# Overflight Considerations Over Indian Reservations

## The short version

Federally recognized tribes **cannot legally restrict airspace** — the FAA has exclusive authority over U.S. navigable airspace. However:

- Federal regulation (14 CFR 93 Subpart U) restricts flight over the Grand Canyon area, which overlaps Hualapai, Havasupai, and portions of Navajo lands.
- FAA **Advisory Circular 91-36D** recommends VFR pilots maintain at least **2,000 ft AGL** over noise-sensitive areas, which the FAA defines to include tribal lands and culturally or historically significant sites.
- Several tribes have published **voluntary overflight-avoidance requests**. These are not enforceable airspace restrictions, but respecting them is courteous and supports continued tribal-FAA cooperation.

## Authoritative references

### AC 91-36D — Visual Flight Rules (VFR) Flight Near Noise-Sensitive Areas

Recommends that pilots of civil aircraft fly no lower than 2,000 ft AGL over noise-sensitive areas unless otherwise required for safety, emergency, or in compliance with ATC. "Noise-sensitive areas" includes National Parks, National Wildlife Refuges, National Monuments, Wilderness Areas, and **tribal lands and sites of cultural or historical significance**.

Source: FAA AC 91-36D (current revision). https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars

### 14 CFR 91.119(c) — Minimum Safe Altitudes

Over other than congested areas, an altitude of 500 ft above the surface. Over open water or sparsely populated areas, aircraft may not operate closer than 500 ft to any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure. Read with AC 91-36D when operating over sensitive tribal sites.

### 49 USC 40128 — National Parks Air Tour Management Act (NPATMA)

Commercial air tour operators conducting flights over National Parks **or tribal lands within or abutting a National Park** must operate under an Air Tour Management Plan (ATMP) or a voluntary agreement. Affected tribes are consulted as part of the FAA/NPS plan development. This primarily affects tour operators, not general Part 91 pilots.

Source: 49 USC § 40128.

### FAA Order 1210.20 — Tribal Consultation Policy

The FAA consults with federally recognized tribes on airspace actions, airport projects, and other matters that may affect tribal sovereignty, lands, sacred sites, or resources.

Source: FAA Order 1210.20.

## What this means in practice for a Part 91 pilot

1. **There is no nationwide "tribal airspace" restriction.** You may legally overfly any reservation at any altitude that complies with 14 CFR 91.119.
2. **Courtesy matters.** The FAA explicitly asks pilots to give tribal lands the same 2,000 ft AGL buffer suggested over parks and refuges, especially near pueblos, sacred sites, and villages.
3. **Check current charts and the Chart Supplement.** Sectional charts carry printed advisory notes for specific tribal areas (e.g., Taos Pueblo area). The Chart Supplement (A/FD) carries airport-specific notes.
4. **Check NOTAMs before flight.** Occasional temporary flight restrictions are issued over tribal events, wildfires, or other activities on tribal lands.
5. **Tour operators have additional rules.** If you're conducting commercial air tours over or near a National Park that abuts tribal land, 14 CFR 91.119(c) and NPATMA apply — you need an LOA, OpSpec, or ATMP.

## If you land on tribal land

Federal airspace preemption protects your right to fly over a reservation. It does **not** protect your aircraft once the wheels stop. Tribal governments are sovereign over tribal land; tribal police and tribal courts have jurisdiction on the ground. This matters when an emergency landing, or any landing, puts you on tribal territory.

### The airspace / ground split

- **While airborne:** FAA has exclusive authority over U.S. navigable airspace. Tribal ordinances asserting overflight restrictions are not enforceable against pilots under federal law.
- **Once on the ground:** tribal law applies. Tribal police may respond. Tribal courts have civil jurisdiction over non-members in some circumstances (see *Montana v. United States*, 450 U.S. 544). Aircraft recovery requires tribal government coordination and can take days to weeks.

### Tribal overflight assertions

Several federally recognized tribes have tribal codes or resolutions that assert minimum-altitude restrictions over their lands. None of these are FAA-enforced. Known documented cases:

- **Red Lake Band of Chippewa Indians, Minnesota** — Resolution No. 59-78 (1978): prohibits aircraft operations below 20,000 ft over the reservation.
- **Snoqualmie Indian Tribe, Washington** — Tribal Airspace Protection Act (27 June 2024): 500 ft AGL minimum generally, 2,000 ft above highest obstacle within 3,000 ft of Snoqualmie Falls; civil penalties up to $1,000.
- **Hualapai Indian Tribe, Arizona** — asserted airspace authority up to 15,000 ft AGL after a 2009 paraglider landing incident. Dispute settled.
- **Taos Pueblo, New Mexico** — voluntary FAA / Taos Pueblo MOA (December 2011) asking pilots to remain above 5,000 ft AGL over the World Heritage Site. Shown on the Albuquerque sectional.
- **Navajo Nation, Arizona/New Mexico/Utah** — prohibits aircraft and UAV operations over specified tribal parks (Monument Valley, Lake Powell Navajo Tribal Park, Tseyi Dine Heritage Area, Little Colorado River Gorge) without an approved flight plan.

These assertions do not make the airspace off-limits. They do indicate how the tribal government will view an incident that puts an aircraft on the ground.

### Documented incident: Red Lake emergency landing, October 15, 2025

Pilot Darrin Smedsmo, flying a 1946 Stinson 108 from Roseau to Bemidji, Minnesota, experienced engine failure at 3,500 ft over Lower Red Lake. He successfully landed on a road on the Red Lake Indian Reservation. Red Lake Nation impounded the aircraft, citing the 1978 tribal resolution and stating the landing occurred "without prior authorization" with "safety, liability, and resource-protection concerns."

- AOPA requested federal intervention in **December 2025**, arguing federal authority over navigable airspace supersedes tribal jurisdiction and asking the FAA for talks on emergency landings on tribal lands. The FAA has not publicly taken a definitive position.
- In **March 2026**, Red Lake Nation offered a settlement requiring a **$5,000 donation to the Red Lake Boys & Girls Club and a $2,750 towing fee**. Smedsmo rejected the offer and disputed the tribal court's claim he had appeared.
- As of March 2026, the aircraft remained impounded nearly 5 months after the landing. The legal dispute continues.
- The 1978 Red Lake resolution is **not on FAA sectional charts** and the FAA does not enforce it against airspace users.

Sources: AVweb, "Pilot Settlement Tribal Aircraft Seizure" — https://avweb.com/aviation-news/aviation-law/pilot-settlement-tribal-aircraft-seizure/ ; AOPA; MPR News.

### What to take from this

An emergency landing that is entirely lawful under 14 CFR 91.3 can still trigger a months-long aircraft-recovery dispute if it lands on tribal land where the tribe asserts jurisdiction. Federal airspace preemption does not preclude ground-based consequences.

**Before flight:** know where reservation boundaries fall on your route. Give noise-sensitive tribal lands the 2,000 ft AGL AC 91-36D buffer when practical.

**Choosing an emergency landing site:** if you have a real choice (altitude, time, airspeed permitting) and multiple viable sites, a non-reservation field is preferable. Pilot safety remains the first priority — 14 CFR 91.3 and 91.119(b) do not require you to avoid reservations in an emergency.

**If you do land on tribal land:**

1. Safety first — occupants, egress, shutdown, fire risk.
2. Call 911 for medical/rescue; contact ATC or the nearest FAA FSDO as soon as practical.
3. **Contact AOPA Legal Services Plan** before making statements to tribal authorities: https://www.aopa.org/legal-services/emergency-legal-contacts
4. Coordinate recovery through tribal government; tribal permits and/or settlement may be required. Your insurance adjuster cannot access the aircraft without tribal coordination.
5. Document everything — photos, timestamps, witness contacts, interactions with tribal officials.

This document is situational awareness, not legal advice. Consult your insurance carrier and an aviation attorney for specifics.
